Foundation

The Structural Flaw

Before Moxso can make sense, the thing it replaces has to break. Here's where it breaks.

The security industry built its human risk programmes on three premises. They made sense when the category was young. The threat landscape has moved on - and the premises haven't.

01
Risk is evenly distributed across the workforce. It isn't. It concentrates. Roughly 8% of employees account for 80% of incidents. Uniform intervention on a non-uniform population doesn't reduce risk - it redistributes it.
02
Training creates security. It doesn't. Behaviour change within a system does. Training is the activity. Security is the outcome. Confusing the two is the original sin of the SAT category.
03
Completion reflects readiness. It doesn't. It reflects that the programme ran. A 100% completion rate on a programme that doesn't change behaviour tells you the activity happened - not that the risk moved.

The consequence of building on these premises: the system fails not from lack of effort but from lack of structure. Organisations spend on training, measure completion, report to the board, and remain exposed - because the measurement system was designed for compliance, not security.

The core compression. Legacy SAT is a library. Static. You visit it. It doesn't come to you. Moxso is an engine. Continuous. Adaptive. It runs without orchestration and produces proof without ceremony.

Our job is not to explain this flaw. It is to make the buyer recognise it in their own environment - in their own words - before Moxso is ever mentioned.

Regulation

The Regulatory Tailwind

Regulators have already moved. Most buyers haven't caught up yet. That's the window.

NIS2 and DORA don't just require training to run. They require evidence that the training is working. Completion records are no longer sufficient as the primary proof. The regulatory bar has moved from activity to behavioural evidence - and most SAT programmes were built to produce the former, not the latter.

NIS2
Broad EU application. In force October 2024. Requires demonstrable behavioural evidence: incident reporting rates, role-specific training effectiveness, assessed improvement over time. Completion records are not sufficient. Applies to medium and large organisations in covered sectors across the EU.
DORA
Financial sector only. In force January 2025. Mandates compulsory ICT resilience training for every employee and every member of senior management, with evidence it is working. The stricter of the two. Apply only to financial sector prospects - banks, insurers, investment firms, payment providers. Never cite DORA outside financial services.

Critical scoping rule. DORA is financial sector only. Citing DORA to a non-financial prospect immediately undermines credibility. For all other sectors, NIS2 is the frame. Know the difference before any regulatory conversation.

The operative question for every regulatory conversation: Is your current programme producing the behavioural evidence regulators are asking for - or is it producing completion records? It's a question worth exploring together before the audit does it for you.

Why tension works - the four-stage cognitive event

Tension isn't a stylistic choice. It produces a specific sequence of events inside the reader's mind. Understanding the mechanism makes it easier to use intentionally - and to recognise when something isn't landing. All four stages must happen in order - skip one and the event collapses into information transfer, which is useful but not category-building.

01
Recognition The reader encounters something familiar - a situation they've lived in, a system they trust, a problem they've normalised. No friction. No challenge yet. This stage earns the right to what comes next. It also explains why Moxso never attacks the incumbent directly: attacking something the reader has invested in triggers defence. Recognising it as legitimate - and then revealing its flaw - bypasses that defence entirely.
02
The Crack A single word, phrase, or reframe introduces productive discomfort. Something in the familiar picture is slightly wrong - not catastrophically, just wrong enough to pause. The crack doesn't explain itself. It doesn't need to. The reader feels it before they can articulate it. "Completion proves the programme ran. It says nothing about what changed." The word "nothing" is the crack. The reader already knew this. They just hadn't said it.
03
The Shift The frame moves. The reader sees the same situation from a different angle, and the new angle reveals something the old one concealed. This is not new information added to an existing structure - it is the structure itself being replaced. The instinct here is to over-explain the shift. Resist it. The shift only has lasting effect if the reader makes it themselves. Show the new frame. Don't guide them through it.
04
The Realisation The reader arrives at a conclusion you didn't state. They've travelled from familiar → uncomfortable → reframed → understood - and the destination feels like their own idea, not yours. This is the event that builds a category. Not persuasion. Recognition. "They get it. Finally, someone gets it." When a buyer says that, the crack is open and Moxso is already the answer.

The operating principle. Never explain the idea. Explain why the old explanation no longer holds. If a sentence is explaining the idea - how the product works, what adaptive means, why continuous is better than periodic - it is informing. Useful. Not category building. If it exposes the flaw in the existing explanation - it is reframing. That is the work.

Voice & Language

The voice is not a style. It is a consequence of believing something true and refusing to talk around it.

Most B2B writing fails because it starts with the product and works outward. Moxso writing starts inside the reader's experience and works inward. The product is not the point of the sentence. It is the relief at the end of a held breath.

The best test for a piece of Moxso writing is not "does this explain us correctly?" It is: does the reader feel seen before they feel sold to? If the answer is no - the direction is wrong. Start again from inside the experience.

Every piece of Moxso writing is built inside-out.

Active over passive. Always.

ICP

Who To Sell To

Not defined by size or sector. Defined by structural readiness to act on human risk.

Every organisation faces human-driven attacks. 94% report at least one phishing incident annually. The threat is universal. Which means threat exposure is useless as a qualification signal - it tells you nothing about who can actually buy.

The exposure paradox. Exposure is universal. Readiness is not. The real divide in this market is not industry, size, or geography - it's structural. Only organisations with the right foundational conditions can take meaningful action on human risk. Those conditions are: clear ownership, basic behavioural visibility, some operational capacity, and cultural readiness to move beyond awareness toward behaviour change.

The ICP in one sentence: Structurally mid-market organisations that are security-accountable, operationally constrained, and under rising human-driven incident pressure - where outcomes are required but programme ownership, governance cadence, and security-operations maturity do not exist.

The four archetypes - ICP verdict on each

The market divides into four structural archetypes. They appear consistently across industries and regions. Two are Core ICP. Two are not. Know which is which before the first call.

A2
Operationally-stretched, security-accountable - Core ICP A small IT/security team accountable for outcomes, overwhelmed by incidents, with near-zero bandwidth. They see the problem but cannot run programmes. Job: "Reduce human-driven incidents without adding operational work." Moxso was explicitly built for this. Ownership exists. Capacity doesn't. Automation is the unlock.
A3
Security-maturing without stable governance - Core ICP A growing security function with visibility into human risk. They can see patterns and problem users but execution is fragile - governance cycles are inconsistent, champions burn out, progress stalls when priorities shift. Job: "Help us turn insight into action without running a full programme." Same core job as A2, slightly more maturity.
A1
Compliance-heavy, low-maturity - Not ICP Security is administrative, not operational. Training is a checkbox. The job is "demonstrate reasonable steps" - passing audits, satisfying regulators, avoiding friction. They will not use insights or follow-up actions. Structural foundations are absent. Disqualify. If the job is to demonstrate effort → disqualify. If the job is to reduce incidents under constraints → qualify.
A4
Enterprise security organisations - Not ICP today SOC analysts, pipelines, dashboards, playbooks. Full operationalisation of HRI workflows. The QBR structure

01
Open with the score, not the activity "Your Human Resilience Score at the start of Q[X] was [N]. It's now [N+]. Here's what drove that movement." This is the first sentence of every QBR. Not "we ran 14 simulations." Not "completion rate is 94%." The score. Always the score first.

Name the risk that moved - and why Which categories improved? Which are still elevated? Set the next 90-day target Every QBR ends with a specific, measurable goal for the next quarter. Not "continue to improve" - a number.
